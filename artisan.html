Decline in Opportunity for Artisans

While many people in antebellum America had increased opportunity due to the market revolution, many artisans and farmers were forced out of their careers into low paying, unskilled jobs.

Prior to the market revolution, most manufacturing jobs were done by artisans. Artisans would own private shops or even work out of their homes, making products by hand. These “Master craftsman” would employ other artisans, known as journeymen and artisans. Journeymen were experienced craftsman who lacked capital to run their own shops, and would work in hopes of saving enough to do so later in life. Apprentices, who were typically teenage boys first entering the trade, would be taught through experience while also running errands for their employers. David Johnson recalls apprentices in the shoemaking business:

“A boy while learning his trade was called a ‘seamster;’ that is, he sewed the shoes for his master, or employer, or to use one of the technicalities of the ‘craft,’ he ‘worked on the seam.’“(Johnson, David)

However, this system of manufacturing saw a steep decline in the early 19th century. In it’s place arose the “Putting-out-system.” This system was characterized by “Machineless Factories” where experienced manufacturers were replaced by multiple unskilled workers. Apprentices and Journeymen were forced to join these low-wage workers while their shop-owners became “Foremen.” This change also meant that workers no longer lived in the homes of their masters and masters no longer assumed paternalistic responsibility for their workers. The employee-employer relationship suddenly became significantly less personal and most importantly, work became subject to supply and demand. “Old Apprentice,” writing into the New York Observer in 1826 laments:

“These men prefer that the boys should not be bound as in that case there is nothing binding upon them. If the boy is taken sick or is guilty of misconduct, he can be turned adrift upon the public or his friends, and no responsibility attached to the employer.”(pseudonym)

This system then gave way to the factory system, in which significantly more work was performed in one location, with the help of newly invented machines. One of the first examples were the textile mills of Boston, specifically the Lowell Mill and the women it employed. Workers in these mills required little to no skill and therefore could be anyone. This meant that employers would hire those willing to work for the lowest wage and forced those looking for a job to accept those low wages, or have no job at all. While this system opened up independence and opportunity for Immigrants, Women and others, former journeymen and apprentices were forced into a situation significantly worse than what they had before. So much so that it caused conflict between these groups. Racism and Slavery were reinforced by the fear that Wages would be driven ever lower. As one man writes in 1838:

“I am aware that most of you have too strong antipathy to encourage the masonry and carpentry trades of your poor white brothers, that your predilections for giving employment in your line of business to ebony workers have either so cheapened the white man’s labor, or expatriated hence with but a few solitary exceptions, all the white masons and carpenters of this town.”(Flournoy, J. J.)

While the market revolution was mostly a positive event in America’s history, It is important to recall those adversely affected and the role they played in history. History may be written by winners, often the most can be learned from the losers.
